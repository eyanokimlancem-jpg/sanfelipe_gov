Summary

1. Introduction

Under Republic Act No. 9003, otherwise known as the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act, all dumpsites shall be closed, and open dumping as well as controlled dumpsites are strictly prohibited. The law mandates the formulation and implementation of a Solid Waste Management Plan to establish an effective system for waste reduction and proper waste handling.

In consideration of directives issued by the Environmental Bureau of the DENR and the Office of the Ombudsman, the Municipality recognizes the urgent need to formulate a Municipal Ecological Solid Waste Management Plan to ensure compliance and environmental sustainability.

1.1 Purpose

The Municipality’s vision for Solid Waste Management is anchored on the full adoption and implementation of the provisions of RA 9003. One of the major challenges in implementing the law is achieving social acceptability and active participation of the community.

Addressing this challenge requires the conduct of extensive information and education campaigns, as well as the dedication and initiative of concerned officials, sectors, and partner agencies to ensure effective implementation.

The intent of RA 9003 and its impact on Solid Waste Management are especially significant in the practice of segregation at source, which must primarily be implemented at the household level.

Approach

Waste Characterization Studies shall be conducted at the barangay level to generate accurate and reliable data for planning and projection purposes. These studies shall be carried out by the Technical Working Group (TWG) or the Municipal Solid Waste Management Board in coordination with barangay officials.

2. Municipal Profile

2.1 Location

The Municipality of San Felipe is located in the central part of the Province of Zambales. It is bounded on the north by the Municipality of Cabangan, on the south by the Municipality of San Narciso, on the west by the Philippine Sea, and on the east by the Municipalities of San Marcelino and Botolan. San Felipe is approximately 200 kilometers away from Manila by land transportation.

The municipality has a total land area of 11,160 hectares (or 111.60 square kilometers). In comparison, the Province of Zambales has a total land area of 3,713.40 square kilometers. San Felipe therefore accounts for approximately 3.10 percent of the total land area of the province.

2.2 History

The Municipality of San Felipe was one of the early villages along the coastal region of Zambales during the pre-Spanish period. The first inhabitants were Malays who preferred to settle in the lowlands, while Negrito and Aeta communities occupied the upland and hinterland areas. Early Ilocano settlers eventually arrived and established communities in Hindol, later known as Sindol, and in a village further south known as Bobulon.

Hindol was a Zambal Aeta name derived from a tree that was once abundant in the area, while Bobulon was also named after a type of tree. Another account suggests that the name Bobulon originated from the favorable winds (bolun) experienced by the first Ilocano settlers sailing from Paoay, Ilocos Norte to the area. The name Bobulon was officially changed to San Felipe when the town was founded in 1853.

The name San Felipe was derived from one of the “saintly” brothers from the Ilocos region—Marcelino, Antonio, Narciso, and Felipe—who settled in different pueblos in the southern part of Bobulon. Marcelino settled in what is now San Marcelino, Antonio in San Antonio, Narciso in San Narciso, and Felipe in the area now known as San Felipe. As a result, the native inhabitants of these towns are predominantly Ilocanos from Ilocandia.

For several years, San Felipe has been recognized as the “Greenest and Cleanest” municipality in the province, and later in Region III and the entire country, due to its effective clean-and-green initiatives and solid waste management programs. Today, San Felipeneans continue to uphold this legacy through collective efforts led by the local government, barangay units, and active community participation.

Transportation

Profile of the Existing Situation

The Municipality of San Felipe is connected to and accessible by both land and water transportation. Major land routes include the Sta. Cruz–San Felipe route passing through the provincial capital town of Iba, Zambales, extending southward to the City of Olongapo and Metro Manila via the Subic Bay Freeport Zone. These routes provide vital access for the movement of people, goods, and services within and outside the municipality.

Roads and Bridges

At present, San Felipe has an estimated total road length of approximately 34.7 kilometers, classified into four (4) categories: national, provincial, municipal, and barangay roads. Provincial roads comprise the largest portion, with an approximate length of 15.65 kilometers, followed by barangay roads with a total length of 9.28 kilometers. Road pavements within the municipality consist of concrete, asphalt, and dirt/earth fill surfaces, with dirt or earth-filled roads covering a total length of 17.23 kilometers.

Transport

The riding public of the municipality and its ability to move and circulate goods freely depends on private and government owned vehicles such as buses, mini-buses, bancas and tricycles. From the town proper to barangays, jeeps and tricycles are available. Approximately about 20 jeeps plying around and in nearby municipalities and about 900 registered tricycle units although the number increases from time to time. Buses plying to Sta. Cruz-Metro Manila and Manila to Sta. Cruz are available as early as 3:00 o’clock in the morning up to late 11:00 0’clock in the evening. For shorter distance, tricycles are available to transport peoples and goods.


The other means of transport services is the water transportation that is available to coastal barangays as well as in barangays located in islands of nearby municipalities. Motorboats and bancas are the only means of transportation used by fisherfolks in their fishing livelihood as well as to bring their catch and products to the market.


2.5 Physical Characteristics

Topography

The Municipality of San Felipe has varied terrain characterized by flat lands, upland and rolling areas, as well as forested and mountainous regions. Approximately 40 percent of the municipality consists of flat lands located primarily on the western side of the mountain ranges, making these areas suitable for agriculture, residential, commercial, and industrial development.

About 15 percent of the total land area is composed of upland and rolling terrain, while the remaining 45 percent comprises forested and mountainous areas. The western portion of the mountain range is generally flat and gently slopes toward the west, ultimately ending at the South China Sea.

Barangay Boundaries

San Felipe is politically subdivided into eleven (11) barangays. Six (6) barangays are located within the town proper (poblacion), while five (5) barangays are situated outside the poblacion and are considered rural areas.

Among these barangays, Balincaguing is the most remote, located in the eastern portion of the municipality approximately 8.15 kilometers from the poblacion center and the national road. Barangay Maloma is situated about 5.10 kilometers from the poblacion center and lies along the national highway, while Barangay Sindol is only 1.5 kilometers from the town center and is also accessible via the national road.

Climate and Rainfall

The municipality experiences two distinct seasons, similar to other municipalities in the province: the wet (rainy) season and the dry season. The wet season generally occurs from May to October, while the remainder of the year is relatively dry with occasional rainfall dispersed throughout the province.

Typhoons occasionally affect the municipality; however, the mountain ranges on the eastern side provide natural protection. In most cases, damage to crops and properties is primarily caused by heavy rainfall and flooding rather than strong winds.

Type of Soil

San Felipe has six major types of soil distributed across the municipality. Antipolo clay comprises approximately 26 percent of the land area, located mainly in the eastern and parts of the western portions. This soil type is suitable for lowland and upland rice, vegetables, and fruit trees.

Around 16 percent of the municipality consists of Angeles sand, which is suitable for lowland rice, vegetables, sugarcane, and root crops. Angeles fine sand accounts for about 15 percent of the land area, found primarily in the southwestern portion, and is suitable for lowland rice, root crops, vegetables, corn, and sugarcane.

Beach sand comprises approximately 14 percent of the land area, located along the western coastal portion of the municipality. This soil type is suitable for trees such as giant ipil-ipil, agoo, kakawati, and similar species. Another 14 percent consists of Cabangan clay loam found in the northwestern portion, which is suitable for lowland rice, vegetables, and sugarcane.

Cabangan swampy loam accounts for about 25 percent of the land area and is found in the central portion of the municipality, particularly between mountain ranges and small valleys. This soil type is suitable for lowland and upland rice, vegetables, and fruit trees.

Following the eruption of Mt. Pinatubo in June 1991, many areas were covered with one to two feet of lahar deposits. Agricultural lands remain under rehabilitation, with ongoing recovery efforts utilizing bulldozers and tractors.

Water Resources

Several rivers originating from the eastern mountain ranges converge into Apga Lake, where the Apga irrigation system is located. Other significant water bodies include Sukit Lake and Nilasin Lake. Major rivers include the Sto. Tomas River at Sitio Macolcol, Bangar River, Gabor River, the old Sto. Tomas River, and the Maloma River.

Potable drinking water sourced from mountain springs serves rural communities in Sitio Apga, Nanguloan, and nearby sitios. These rivers also function as natural drainage outlets during the rainy season.

Timber Resources

In the past, San Felipe was one of the timber-producing municipalities in Zambales, where tree species such as narra, yakal, tindalo, maupajo, calantas, apitong, and others were harvested. One major cause of forest denudation was unregulated logging by lumber concessionaires who failed to replace cut trees.

As a result of careless logging practices, forest areas became denuded. During the wet season, flooding occurs, posing risks to life and property due to reduced forest cover.

Mineral Resources

The mountain ranges on the eastern side of the municipality are rich in mineral resources. Ongoing exploration has identified the presence of asbestos, chromite, and copper deposits in these areas.

Following the 1991 eruption of Mt. Pinatubo and the continuous flow of lahar through major river systems such as the Maloma River, Maculcol–Sto. Tomas River, and Gabor River, extensive deposits of lahar sand containing magnetite have accumulated along these waterways.

Non-Mineral Resources

Non-mineral resources in the municipality include sand, gravel, earth, stones, and boulders. Bamboo, boho, rattan, and cogon grass grow abundantly in forest areas and are commonly used by residents as indigenous building materials. Rattan is also utilized in the production of furniture and handicrafts.

Flora and Fauna

The favorable climate and geographic features of San Felipe provide excellent conditions for plant growth and agricultural productivity. Common fruit trees include mango, star apple, coconut, and guava. Bamboo, boho, rattan, and cogon are widely used for housing materials and livelihood purposes.

Orchids such as waling-waling, along with ornamental and flowering plants, are propagated to enhance the town’s aesthetic appeal. Agricultural crops such as rice, corn, and vegetables contribute significantly to local economic development. Wild doves and ducks are among the most common fauna, especially with hunting now prohibited.

3. Religion

The San Felipeneans are generally religious and has a deep reverence to their Patron Saint, the Apo san Roque, lover of animals and the environment. Religion consist of the Methodist, Iglesia Filipina Independiente, the Roman Catholic Church denominations. There are other Christian religions with their churches established around the municipality such as the Jesus is Lord Church, Mormon Church and others. The local parish of the Catholic Church is dedicated to the Patron Saint, the Apo San Roque. San Felipe, Zambales is also the site of the Cathedral of the Iglesia Filipina Independiente where the Bishop of the said religious sect hold its seat.

4. Festivals, Fiestas And Community Activities

Panagkakadua-an Festival

To promote the unique brand of unity, reunions and family bonds of the people, the Panagkakadua-an Festival to be held a week before the Holy Week every three (3) years which has become a venue to promote products such as the longganisa and the chicharon (bagnet) among others.

Community Affairs

Conducted every December in celebration of the community Christmas affair, it has been an opportunity to gather the people in an assembly, recognize outstanding achievers of the community and give honor to various sectors including the balikbayans.


Foundation Day

Held every 1st of March, it is an opportunity for every San Felipenean to look back to its humble beginnings and cherish its successes and accomplishment in songs, dances and tributes ultimately to deepen the sense of pride to its people for its history and heritage.
